Te Whakarāpopototanga  |  Executive Summary

Your Ngā Pou Mana Strategic Plan 2025–2030 provides a confident steer for your direction ahead. It gives Ngā Pou Mana a clear identity as an independent kaupapa Māori peak body, with three well-chosen Pou, and an honest risk read.

Our verdict is that this strategy is ready to proceed to the next stages: the mandate, direction and constituency are all in place.  The strategy work can now move to strengthening its detail.

The main opportunities ahead for the board relate to the measurement and delivery layer (including SMART end-goals, KPIs, ownership, review rhythm, sequencing) and deepening the 'te ao Māori framing' into a working conceptual framework. This was an area that the organisation itself has identified in our scoping kōrero so far.

Te Poutama Rautaki  |  Strategy Completeness (12 steps)

Te Whare Hukahuka considers strategy through the lens of a Strategic Planning Poutama, with 12 steps. 

Assessing your current strategy against the 12 steps, it demonstrates that the directional steps are fairly strong; but the delivery steps need to be tightened further. Several steps would be suitable content for a board workshop.

How to read the Poutama: The 12 steps sit in order and take the shape of a poutama – this reflects that they are sequential. Together they signal what a complete Strategic Plan will convey.

Goals are specific and actionable. The strategies each begin with verbs (e.g. Strengthen, Explore, Enhance, Support); but none says how much, or by when.
Goals express end outcomes rather than the means. Some Tohu Angitū are reflecting true end-states ("sustainable and independent financial systems"); but others describe the activity (the means).

Ngā Āhuatanga o te Angitū   |  Determinants of success (six tests)

This section assesses the extent to which your Strategic Plan creates the conditions for six kaupapa Māori determinants of success to emerge.

These six determinants are drawn from Pakake Winiata’s 'model for success' (set out in his Master of Mātauranga Māori thesis) - and were found to be present in successful kaupapa Māori such as Whakatupuranga Rua Mano, Te Māori, Te Aurere waka hourua, Waka 1990 Celebrations and Te Wānanga o Raukawa. The model for success predicts that kaupapa Māori with these six determinants present will be successful. We have assessed your Strategic Plan against these six determinants of success.

1. Every section of the iwi are involved in the kaupapa.
The strategy positions itself as a member-centred organisation. It proposes significant consultation. It aims to strengthen partnerships across iwi, tertiary institutions and professional bodies.
However, there is little evidence that different sections of the NPM membership (rangatahi, experienced clinicians, kaumātua, different professions, regions etc.) have ongoing roles in shaping or delivering the strategy beyond consultation.
2. Rangatahi are involved and strengthened.
This is one of the strongest aspects of the NPM strategy. Rangatahi leadership pathways, mentorship, tuakana–teina programmes and visibility of emerging Māori allied health leaders are explicit strategic priorities with associated success indicators. Beyond simply engaging rangatahi, the Strategic Plan seeks to intentionally grow future leadership.
3. Māori knowledge is actively restored and strengthened.
This is strong. An entire Strategic Pou is devoted to Māori knowledge, including research, bilingual resources, cultural capability, rongoā, scholarships and policy influence. Rather than merely preserving knowledge, the strategy seeks to actively generate new mātauranga and strengthen its application within allied health.
4. The kaupapa is spiritually uplifting.
The NPM strategy is clearly values-based and grounded in Te Tiriti, He Whakaputanga, Tika, Pono and Aroha. It speaks strongly about identity, mana and tino rangatiratanga. However, it says relatively little about intentionally cultivating the spiritual wellbeing of members, whānau or the organisation itself. Wairua feels like an implicit foundation rather than an explicit strategic outcome.
5. The leaders are determined to make the kaupapa succeed.
Leadership is highly visible throughout the strategy. Governance capability, succession planning, advocacy, board development, transparent decision-making and Indigenous leadership are recurring themes. There is strong evidence of committed leadership, although accountability mechanisms for leaders could be strengthened further.
6. There is external support and sponsorship.
The strategy recognises that long-term success depends on strong partnerships, diversified funding, collaboration and financial independence. Risks around funding continuity are acknowledged alongside mitigation strategies. While the emphasis is on independence rather than sponsorship alone, the underlying determinant is well addressed.

Overall, your Strategic Plan 2025–2030 demonstrates strong alignment with 4 of the 6 determinants of successful kaupapa Māori. The two areas with the greatest opportunity to improve are around community involvement (determinant 1) and the kaupapa being spiritually uplifting (determinant 4). Improving these are less about adding initiatives and more about strengthening the philosophy of the strategy.

Kaupapa Māori conceptual framework

The Strategic Plan is grounded in kaupapa Māori, and this is more than just decorative. The board is making tino rangatiratanga a live decision rule ( "accepting only kaupapa-aligned projects"). The three Pou are given Māori headings. A full Pou (Te Pou Mana Māori) is dedicated to mātauranga, rongoā and reo, making Māori knowledge a strategic priority in its own right. 


The conceptual layer could be strengthened – including a whakapapa for the three Pou, whakataukī or pūrākau anchoring each, and an overall Karakia Rautaki. These would make the overall framework itself Māori rather than having a standard strategy with Māori headings. The board has already suggested this as an area to deepen the strategy already. This is a clear opportunity for a board rautaki workshop.

Kupu Tohutohu  |  Top Recommendations

Based on everything we assessed, below are our top recommendations to strengthen your Strategic Plan.

1

Measurement clarity

For each Pou, have 1-3 SMART end-goals.  Translate the current Success Indicators into actual board-ready KPIs that the board can monitor.
To get this startedAs a next step, take the strongest indicator per Pou and write a measurable target for it (for example, "X by December 2027"), then agree how the board wants to see the reporting against this.
2

Pou ownership

For each Pou (or possibly each Initiative), name an owner (generally the CEO, or a board committee or partner org). Note what they are responsible for doing, for reporting, and the delegated authority they have. 
To get this startedAt your next board hui agree the above details, or ask your CEO to draft this and present their recommendation. 
3

Review rhythm

The board should set a regular cycle for reviewing progress against the plan as well as whether the plan is still a fair navigational tool.  This will help ensure the strategy stays a living tool after ratification and in being tracked against. Review should be at least six-monthly.
A practical approachEmbed a standing strategy item into the board's meeting agenda every six months, with a short update per Pou from each owner.
4

Confirm the current position (including lessons from previous strategy)

The board should undertake or commission a short review of the 2019–2024 Strategic Plan to identify what was achieved; what should carry through into the new strategy; what things blocked progress; and what work the organisation ended up doing that never actually appeared in the strategy document. This can then become part of a current position stocktake of where the organisation is at today – so that the 2030 goals have a stated starting point.
To get this startedHave the CEO undertake this and distribute it as pre-reading before the board's rautaki workshop. Have one page answering the four questions, and one page covering the organisation's current resources and partners.
5

Kaupapa Māori framing into a framework

The board should explore and decide how kaupapa Māori concepts and mātauranga can be imbued into the strategy – e.g. through whakataukī, pūrākau, or Karakia Rautaki. This will ensure your strategy truly carries a wairua Māori rather than just be 'Māori-labelled'.
One way to beginHold a dedicated wānanga around to the question "what tūpuna kōrero or whare wānanga lessons connect to each Pou?". Our Pou Amorangi of Te Whare Hukahuka could also lead the board through a Karakia Rautaki process to imbue your strategy with tapu and mana.
